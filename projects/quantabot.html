Overview

A retrieval-augmented system that lets clinicians ask grounded questions across long dermatology papers - and cites the exact passages that support every answer.

Dermatology research is fragmented across long, dense manuscripts, and the LLM tools clinicians reached for first (general-purpose chatbots) hallucinated citations and lost context across multi-page papers. Quanta is a Python application built around a layered LangChain retrieval pipeline — query expansion, hybrid semantic + BM25 search, MMR diversification, cross-encoder reranking, and parent-document context expansion — paired with a Streamlit interface for clinician use. The system was tested with 10+ clinicians across 55+ manuscripts, cut literature-review time by ~75%, and produced a preprint on bioRxiv (2025.08.14.670384v1) with abstract accepted at KSID × ISID APAC 2026.

The Problem

Dermatologists couldn't trust the AI tools they were reaching for first.

Clinical researchers in dermatology review large collections of long, dense manuscripts to stay current — but the obvious LLM-based shortcuts had two failure modes. General chatbots hallucinated citations (claiming a paper said something it didn't, or inventing references entirely). Naive vector-similarity retrieval also returned passages that sounded topically related but weren't actually evidence for the user's question. For clinical use, "sounds right" isn't enough: every claim needs to point at a real passage, and the retrieval has to surface the actually relevant one, not just the topically similar one.

Product Vision

A tool a clinician can drop a folder of papers into, ask natural-language questions, and get back answers grounded in specific passages they can verify in one click.

The target isn't "better than nothing" — it's "good enough that a researcher would actually use it instead of skimming PDFs themselves." That meant grounding every answer in citations, handling long-document context without losing fidelity, and validating against a benchmark that resembles real clinical reasoning (MedQA / USMLE) rather than just retrieval-flavored toy tasks.

Core architecture

Layered Retrieval Pipeline with Document-Size-Adaptive Branching

Documents are chunked and embedded with Sentence-BERT (MPNet by default, BGE configurable via env var). At query time, MultiQueryRetriever uses GPT-4 to expand the user's question into multiple sub-queries. The pipeline then branches by document size: small corpora (~20 chunks) use ContextualCompressionRetriever; larger ones use EnsembleRetriever combining vector similarity with BM25 keyword search. Results pass through MMR diversification and a cross-encoder reranker, then ParentDocumentRetriever expands the top chunks back into their parent context windows before being passed to GPT-4 for generation. The README explicitly separates retrieval from generation as a design principle, which lets the system diagnose whether failures are retrieval or LLM bottlenecks.

My Contribution

Sole engineer and first author — built the retrieval architecture, validated it statistically, and shipped the clinician-facing app.

I owned the full system end-to-end: choosing retrieval components and the order they compose in, deciding the document-size branching threshold, designing the evaluation framework (including the retrieval-contains-answer diagnostic that disambiguates retrieval vs. generation failures), and building the Streamlit interface clinicians actually used. Dr. Lam Tsoi's group provided clinical guidance and access to the dermatology manuscript corpus; the system design was mine.

What I worked on

  • Retrieval architecture. Designed and implemented the layered pipeline (multi-query expansion → hybrid semantic + BM25 → MMR → cross-encoder reranking → parent-document expansion). Chose the document-size branching threshold based on empirical chunk-count distribution across the corpus.
  • Evaluation framework. Built the MedQA (USMLE) benchmark integration and a retrieval-contains-answer diagnostic that separates "the retriever missed it" from "the LLM saw the right context but answered wrong." Archived earlier TF-IDF vs. SBERT and BEIR/NFCorpus experiments for reproducibility.
  • Hallucination guardrails. Engineered the citation-grounding flow so every generated answer surfaces the specific passages it pulled from — making clinical verification a one-click action.
  • Clinician-facing app and preprint. Built the Streamlit interface (handling uploads, multi-document context, conversation memory), tested it with 10+ clinicians across 55+ manuscripts, and wrote the bioRxiv preprint (first author).

Key Achievements

A clinically grounded RAG system that statistically outperforms the vector-similarity baseline and runs at ~97% lower memory cost.

The statistical validation matters more than the headline numbers. Many RAG papers report that their system "performs better" without statistical significance testing; Quanta's F1 (p = 0.0165) and NDCG (p = 0.004) gains over a vector-similarity baseline were tested on a 55+ manuscript dermatology corpus and held up under significance testing. The clinician feedback from 10+ users showed a ~75% reduction in literature-review time, and the system has been adopted as a working tool inside the Cutaneous Lab. The work led to a bioRxiv preprint and an accepted abstract at KSID × ISID APAC 2026.

Lessons Learned

Separating retrieval from generation is the single decision that made the whole system debuggable.

Most early failures in RAG systems look the same from the outside — wrong answer, vague citation — and you can spend weeks tuning prompts when the real problem is that the retriever never surfaced the relevant chunk. Building the retrieval-contains-answer diagnostic was the unlock: I could see immediately whether the bottleneck was retrieval (improve search) or generation (improve prompts), and stop tuning one when the other was the actual cause. What I'd do differently: I'd build the evaluation harness before the retrieval pipeline, not after. Several weeks of early work tuning chunk sizes and embedding models would have been redirected if I'd had MedQA-style benchmarks running from day one. I'd also be more aggressive about archiving earlier experiments (TF-IDF, BEIR/NFCorpus) into a structured comparison from the start rather than retroactively.

Where It's Going

Forward Path: Manuscript submission to a peer-reviewed venue, and expansion of the evaluation scope.

Longer Term: The bioRxiv preprint is being revised for journal submission with expanded clinician evaluation. On the technical side, the next priorities are (1) adding citation-faithfulness benchmarks (not just whether the answer is correct, but whether the cited passage actually supports it), (2) testing newer retrievers and rerankers as the LangChain ecosystem updates, and (3) generalizing beyond dermatology to other clinical domains where the same retrieval-vs-generation diagnostic would apply.
