A longitudinal test of a personal question I had during military service.

Overview

Does mandatory military service actually shift career trajectories? A 4-year longitudinal test on the Korean Education Longitudinal Study (KELS) 2021 panel of ~4,000 participants.

The folk wisdom I kept hearing during my own military service was that the two years of mandatory service set Korean men back professionally — that time spent away from school or industry would show up as a measurable disadvantage years later. The opposite claim is also common: that the structure, discipline, and time to think shape people in ways that pay off later. Neither claim was supported by the kind of evidence I'd actually accept. So I pulled four years of the KELS 2021 panel — KEDI's government-funded longitudinal survey of ~4,000 participants — tracked career development indicators across waves for veterans and non-veterans, and applied non-parametric tests to see whether the group difference held up under significance testing.

The Question

Everyone has an opinion about military service and careers. Almost nobody has the data.

Korean men spend roughly two years in mandatory military service in their early twenties. Whether that period sets them back, accelerates them, or has no measurable effect is debated constantly — in family conversations, in policy debates, in HR discussions — almost always on the basis of anecdote rather than longitudinal data. KELS 2021 is a KEDI-administered panel survey that follows the same individuals across years, recording education, employment, and career development indicators. That's exactly the structure needed to test the claim, and the dataset is largely used inside academic publications rather than as a public-facing answer to the question regular people are actually asking.

💡
Research question
Using a real longitudinal panel rather than anecdote, can we measure whether veterans and non-veterans differ in career development over time — and how much of any observed difference is robust to standard non-parametric significance testing?

What I Wanted To Find Out

An evidence-based answer to a question I'd been arguing about with friends and family for years, presented clearly enough that someone without a statistics background could see what the data does and doesn't say.

Korean families make real decisions based on assumed military-service career penalties — timing of grad school applications, internship choices, when to enter the job market. If those decisions are calibrated to an effect that isn't actually there in the data, that's worth knowing. Conversely, if the effect is real, the direction matters: my prior was "service hurts careers." The data ran the other way. Updating publicly on that is the entire point.

Methodology

Longitudinal cohort comparison with non-parametric significance testing

Cleaned and harmonized four waves of KELS 2021 across ~4,000 participants. Stratified the sample into veterans (completed mandatory service within the observation window) and non-veterans (had not served by the end of the observation window). Tracked career development indicators across waves, then applied Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for paired within-individual comparisons across time and Kruskal-Wallis tests for between-group comparisons across veteran-status categories. Non-parametric tests were the appropriate choice because the development-score distributions visibly violated normality (skew and ceiling effects). All analysis was done in a single reproducible Jupyter notebook with Seaborn/Matplotlib for the trajectory figures.

My Contribution

Sole author — formulated the question, pulled and cleaned the panel data, ran the statistics, and wrote up the findings.

This was a one-person project from question to write-up. No advisor, no collaborator, no funding — just a question that had been on my mind during military service and the realization that I had enough evening time to actually answer it from public data.

What I worked on

  • Data ingestion and harmonization. Pulled the KELS 2021 panel from the KEDI distribution site, joined waves on participant ID, harmonized variable codings across years (which silently change in some waves), and constructed the veteran vs. non-veteran stratification.
  • Statistical testing. Chose Wilcoxon and Kruskal-Wallis over parametric alternatives after checking distribution assumptions. Ran paired within-individual tests for trajectory comparisons and group-difference tests for between-cohort effects.
  • Visualization and write-up. Built Seaborn/Matplotlib figures showing the trajectory differences and tail behavior, and wrote up the analysis in the notebook with enough narrative that it could be read like a short paper.

Key Findings

Veterans showed persistently higher career development scores across the observation window — running opposite to my prior.

The notebook closes with a finding I didn't expect to write: in this panel, veterans' career development indicators were not just non-worse than non-veterans' — they were systematically higher across multiple waves, and the difference cleared standard non-parametric significance thresholds. This is a correlational finding, not a causal one (more on that in Lessons Learned), but it's a finding strong enough that "military service measurably hurts career outcomes" is harder to defend as a default assumption than I'd thought going in.

Lessons Learned

The biggest lesson wasn't statistical — it was that I'd been confidently wrong about the answer. The data ran the opposite direction from my prior, and the right response was to update.

What worked: picking non-parametric tests up front saved me from the parametric-tests-on-non-normal-data trap that a lot of social-science analyses fall into. Looking at distributions before choosing tests is unglamorous and matters a lot.

What didn't: the analysis is honest about what it found, but doesn't fully address why. Selection effects are real — the small fraction of Korean men who don't serve are exempted for medical reasons, family hardship, or other circumstances that themselves correlate with career outcomes. A correlational finding from a non-random comparison group is interesting, not conclusive.

What I'd do differently: I'd use a method actually designed for causal inference — propensity-score matching on pre-service covariates, or a difference-in-differences if the panel has a clean pre/post-service split for the same individuals — rather than relying on group comparisons alone. That's the version that would settle the question rather than just push back against the default assumption.

Open Questions

No active continuation. This was a one-shot personal analysis answering a question I'd had during my own service.

If I returned to this, the priorities would be:

  • Causal identification. Propensity-score matching or difference-in-differences to address selection effects rather than relying on group comparisons.
  • Additional KELS waves. Extending the analysis as KEDI releases new waves of the panel.
  • Decomposing the headline difference. Which indicator drives it — wage trajectory, employment stability, self-reported satisfaction, or something else?
