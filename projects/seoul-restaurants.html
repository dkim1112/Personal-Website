Seoul Restaurant Survival

Survival analysis on 528K Seoul restaurant license records — how long restaurants actually live, and what kills them.

TimelineApr - May 2026
CompanyIndependent
RoleAnalyst
StackPython · pandas · Jupyter

Overview

Half of Seoul's restaurants close within 6.1 years — but the naive "closure rate" everyone cites is statistically wrong.

Most reporting on Seoul restaurant survival uses simple closure rates: "X% of restaurants fail." That number ignores restaurants still open (right-censoring) and treats a 1-year-old shop the same as a 10-year-old one. This project applies survival analysis (Kaplan-Meier, log-rank tests, Cox proportional hazards regression) to 528K restaurant license records from all 25 Seoul districts, spanning 1980 to 2026. The corrected estimate — median survival of 6.1 years — is more accurate than the naive "77% failure" framing, and the analysis surfaces three findings that the naive rate hides: where you open matters more than what you cook, the COVID-era cohort is closing measurably faster, and restaurants that survive the first three years tend to persist.

The Problem

"What percentage of restaurants fail?" is the wrong question.

Public reporting on Seoul restaurant survival treats it like a binary: a store either failed or didn't. That framing has two specific statistical problems. First, it ignores right-censoring — 121K of the 528K restaurants in the dataset are still operating, and we don't know how long they'll last. Counting them as "succeeded" inflates the success rate; counting them as "failed" deflates it. Second, treating a 1-year-old restaurant the same as a 10-year-old one ignores exposure time entirely. The right question isn't "how many failed?" — it's "at any given point in time, what's the probability a restaurant is still open?" That's a survival analysis question, not a closure-rate question.

Analytical Goal

An honest, reproducible answer to "how long do Seoul restaurants survive, and what factors actually matter?" — backed by methods strong enough to defend against statisticians, not just impressive-sounding to a casual reader.

The goal wasn't to build a dashboard or a predictive product. It was to do the analysis correctly, communicate it clearly, and document the decisions transparently enough that someone else could replicate or challenge them. The deliverable is a pair of Jupyter notebooks plus an Architecture Decision Record explaining every methodological choice — why Kaplan-Meier over closure rates, why this dataset over alternatives, why a 100K sample for Cox PH instead of the full 528K.

Core architecture

Kaplan-Meier + Cox PH with Cohort Stratification on 528K License Records

Raw data is the 행정안전부 일반음식점 (Ministry of Interior and Safety general restaurant) license dataset from data.go.kr — 2.27M nationwide records, filtered to 528K Seoul records after cleaning. Kaplan-Meier estimators produce unbiased survival curves under right-censoring; log-rank tests compare curves across districts (n=25) and business types (n=15). Cox proportional hazards regression quantifies the simultaneous effect of location and cuisine on closure risk, run on a 100K random sample for memory tractability (sufficient for stable estimates; full 528K caused OOM issues). A cohort analysis splits restaurants by opening decade to isolate the COVID effect — a finding I didn't set out to look for but emerged from the data.

My Contribution

Sole analyst — chose the methodology, found and validated the dataset, ran the analysis, and documented every decision.

I scoped the question, made the call to use survival analysis instead of closure-rate framing, sourced the dataset (pivoting from 골목상권 OA-15577 after discovering it was aggregate-only), ran the cleaning and statistical work, and authored the README, ADRs, and journal. There's no team to credit — the project is end-to-end mine.

What I worked on

  • Methodology selection. Chose Kaplan-Meier + Cox PH over closure-rate reporting after recognizing right-censoring bias (121K still-open restaurants). Documented this as the first Architecture Decision Record.
  • Data sourcing and ingestion. Started with 골목상권 (OA-15577) before discovering it was aggregate-level. Pivoted to the 행정안전부 license dataset (658MB raw CSV) — the only public source with store-level open/close dates. Cleaned 2.27M nationwide records down to 528K Seoul records with quality checks.
  • Statistical analysis. Built KM curves across districts, business types, and opening cohorts; ran log-rank tests and Cox PH regression. Used a 100K random sample for Cox PH after the full dataset caused memory issues — verified sample size produced stable estimates.
  • Communication and documentation. Wrote the README, four ADR-style markdown files (CONTEXT, DECISIONS, JOURNAL, ROADMAP), and a Limitations section listing what the data can't tell us — owner experience, rent, revenue, competition density. The limitations section was deliberate: the right way to defend the analysis is to be explicit about what it doesn't cover.

Key Achievements

A methodologically defensible answer to a question that's usually answered wrong, with three findings that wouldn't appear in a naive closure-rate analysis.

The headline result — median 6.1-year survival — is more accurate than the naive "77% closure rate" framing, but the more interesting findings are downstream. Location effects are large and significant: Jongno-gu restaurants have a 37% lower closure risk (HR = 0.63) than the baseline, while Yangcheon-gu has 22% higher (HR = 1.22), and the median-survival gap across districts (5.3 years) exceeds the gap across cuisine types (4.6 years). The COVID cohort is closing measurably faster: 2020–2024 entrants have a 3-year survival of 59.3% vs. 70.6% for the 2010–2014 cohort — an 11-percentage-point drop that didn't appear in any of the headlines I'd read. Survival is front-loaded: restaurants that make it past three years tend to persist, suggesting the early period is where most of the risk lives.

Lessons Learned

Picking the right statistical tool before running anything is worth more than any amount of clever modeling afterward.

Survival analysis was the unlock. If I'd reported "% closed," I would have stated a ~77% failure rate — which sounds dramatic and is statistically wrong, because it ignores 121K restaurants still being observed. The Kaplan-Meier estimate of 55.9% at five years is both more accurate and more useful for anyone actually deciding whether to open a restaurant. The lesson generalizes: choosing the right tool for the right question is upstream of all the analytical work that follows it. The data-sourcing pivot was the other one. I planned to use 골목상권 (OA-15577) and didn't realize until I'd downloaded it that it was aggregate-level data — counts of stores by district, not individual store records. The 행정안전부 license dataset was the correct source. The lesson: verify what's in a dataset before designing an analysis around it. What I'd do differently: I'd run the Cox PH on the full 528K dataset using chunked or out-of-core methods rather than falling back to a 100K random sample. The sample is statistically defensible, but it's a workaround I'd want to remove if I revisited this.

Where It's Going

Forward Path: The analysis itself is complete and reproducible — the next steps would be extending the scope rather than continuing the current work.

Long Path: Three natural extensions: (1) add covariates the license data doesn't include — rent prices by district, foot traffic, KOSIS economic indicators — to attack the Cox PH concordance of 0.55 (the model explains some variance, but unmeasured factors still drive a lot of survival); (2) extend to other cities or other small-business categories to see whether the location-over-cuisine finding generalizes; (3) time-varying covariates — restaurants opened pre- and post-COVID could be modeled with shock periods explicitly, rather than treated as static cohorts. That said, the project is structured as a self-contained analytical study. None of these extensions are committed work — they're directions someone (me or someone else) could take if the question is worth pushing further.
